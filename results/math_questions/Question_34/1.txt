Question: Suppose that I have an unlimited supply of identical math books, history books, and physics books.  All are the same size, and I have room on a shelf for 8 books.  In how many ways can I arrange eight books on the shelf if no two books of the same type can be adjacent?
Teacher: Let's begin by outlining the problem in our own words:

We have 8 positions on a shelf to fill with books chosen from three types (math, history, physics), with the restriction that no two books of the same type can be next to each other. One common way to approach this is by thinking about it step-by-step along the shelf.

Here’s an outline of the steps you might take:

1. Identify the number of choices for the first position.
2. Recognize that for every subsequent position (positions 2 through 8), you cannot choose the same type as the book immediately before it. This will give you a fixed number of choices for these positions.
3. Use the multiplication principle to multiply the number of choices for each position together to get the total number of arrangements.

Let's start with Step 1: What is the number of choices you have for placing a book in the first position on the shelf? Please share your reasoning for this step.
Student: For the first position on the shelf, I can choose any of the three types of books: math, history, or physics. Since there are no restrictions yet (because it's the first position), I have 3 choices for the first position. 

So, my answer for Step 1 is that there are 3 choices for the first position.

So, my answer for Step 2 is that there are 2 choices for the second position.

Now that you've determined that for the first two positions you have 3 choices for the first position and 2 choices for the second, let's move on to Step 3.

For positions 3 through 8, what are the number of choices for each position given that no two adjacent books can be the same, and how would you arrive at the number of choices for these remaining positions? Please share your reasoning, and remember to consider the relationship between each book and the one immediately before it.
Student: Student: For positions 3 through 8, the reasoning remains consistent with what we established for the first two positions. 

Starting from position 3, since I cannot have the same type of book as the one in position 2, I will again have 2 choices for position 3, just like I did for position 2. This is because each book can only be of a different type than the book immediately before it.
